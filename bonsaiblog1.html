Is Bonsai an Art?


Sujina Dangal

December 26, 2022



Acquired from China and modified by the Japanese, a sophisticated method of nurturing miniature trees in containers developed approximately 1200 years ago surrounding the Kamakura period. An abundance of Chinese cultural symbols was adopted by the Japanese, and among the accepted practices, this delicate tree art known as Bonsai (bonsai, ぼんさい, meaning "planted in a pot"), is one that successfully blended and dented into Japanese culture. In Japan, however, the term may be better recognized as bunjin-sō (文人 Soto), which is a rightful direct translation of "tree art of the people."


During the 17th century, the art of bonsai developed a multitude of styles that underwent various alterations as time passed, closely aligning with the ongoing conditional changes in nature as well. In short, bonsai growth is a miniature, yet precise, symbol of nature in the form of a tree. Species of plants such as pine, maple, fir, juniper, fig, dogwood, and elm are not genetically dwarf plants, however, they may be successfully raised as bonsai plants. Additionally, similar families of these species include weeping pine, red twig maple, and dwarf blue spruce.


To raise a bonsai, healthy growth of the tree is controlled via multiple strategies, including pinching buds, trimming, and reconnecting branches. It is also crucial to accurately measure moderate levels of incorporated fertilizers. Typically, trees begin by growing out of a seed, however, it is also possible to cut a section of a living tree and root it into a new, environmentally nourished, pot to cultivate a new plant. In order to build a stronger root system for convenient transfer to the outdoors, it is best to let the cut portion secure itself in its chosen pot or tray while it is initially produced indoors. Once evidence of bonsai development is comfortably detected, the training process may begin. To prevent the trees from becoming overly stressed or potentially dying, it is critical that the training and transitioning to the outdoors be done gradually and cautiously. The tree is then often pruned and shaped to the owner’s liking, eventually reaching its maximized state at which it can no longer grow or be reformed. This stage, however, can take several years. Finally, it is considered a “finished” bonsai when it is mature enough to survive on its own or without being tended to.


Bonsai is considered a unique form of art that is popularly collected by enthusiastic artists and hobbyists worldwide. It is also commonly featured in homes and gardens due to its aesthetically pleasing design and natural comfort. Still, rearing one is much more than just a simple hobby to those who participate. From the moisture in the soil and containers to the quantities of fed water, a plethora of properties blend together to contribute to a success in this practice. For example, an excess of hydration may ruin the delicate balance by damaging and wearing out the roots of the plant. Therefore, the environment should be meticulously regulated according to its specific needs. At las, among the most significant tools going into this art is patience. It may not seem particularly difficult to learn when observing from the perspective of a novice, but it can be time-consuming, requiring that a participant be prepared for the commitment before beginning. After all, bonsai is an art, and the demand is no less than its other artistical neighbors.
